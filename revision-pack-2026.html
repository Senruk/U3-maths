2.2 Partial Fractions

Question 2.2a Basic5 marks

Express ⁡(5x - 1)/((x - 1)(x - 2))⁡ in partial fractions.

Let (5x-1)/((x-1)(x-2)) = A/(x-1) + B/(x-2)
5x-1 = A(x-2) + B(x-1)
x=1: 5-1 = A(-1) ⇒ A = -4
x=2: 10-1 = B(1) ⇒ B = 9
Answer: 9/(x-2) - 4/(x-1)
M1 for form, M1 for clearing denominators, A1 for A, A1 for B, B1 for final answer

Question 2.2b Intermediate5 marks

Express ⁡(3x2 + 4x + 1)/((x + 1)(x2 + 1))⁡ in partial fractions.

(3x2+4x+1)/((x+1)(x2+1)) = A/(x+1) + (Bx+C)/(x2+1)
3x2+4x+1 = A(x2+1) + (Bx+C)(x+1)
x=-1: 3-4+1 = A(2) ⇒ 0 = 2A ⇒ A=0
Coeff of x2: 3 = A + B ⇒ B=3
Coeff of x: 4 = B + C ⇒ C=1
Answer: 3x/(x2+1) + 1/(x2+1) = (3x+1)/(x2+1)
M1 for form, M1 for clearing, A1 for A, A1 for B, A1 for C

Question 2.2c Advanced7 marks

Express ⁡(2x2 - 5x + 7)/((x - 1)(x2 - x + 1))⁡ in partial fractions, then use the binomial expansion to find the first three terms of the expansion of this expression in ascending powers of 1/x.

Partial fractions: A/(x-1) + (Bx+C)/(x2-x+1)

= 4/(x-1) - (2x+3)/(x2-x+1)

2.6 Proof by Contradiction

Question 2.6a Basic4 marks

Prove by contradiction that there is no greatest even integer.

Assume there is a greatest even integer, call it N.
Then N+2 is also even (since adding 2 to an even number gives an even number).
But N+2 > N, which contradicts N being the greatest even integer.
Therefore our assumption is false – there is no greatest even integer.
M1 for assumption, M1 for contradiction, A1 for clear contradiction, A1 for conclusion

Question 2.6b Intermediate5 marks

Prove by contradiction that √3 is irrational.

Assume √3 = p/q where p,q are coprime integers, q ≠ 0.
Square: 3 = p2/q2 ⇒ p2 = 3q2
So p2 is divisible by 3 ⇒ p is divisible by 3. Let p = 3k.
(3k)2 = 3q2 ⇒ 9k2 = 3q2 ⇒ q2 = 3k2
So q is also divisible by 3, contradicting coprimality.
Therefore √3 is irrational.
M1 for assumption, M1 for p2=3q2, A1 for p divisible by 3, A1 for q divisible by 3, A1 for contradiction

Question 2.7b Intermediate6 marks

The sum of the first n terms of a series is given by Sn = 2n2 + 5n.
(a) Find the nth term un.
(b) Prove the series is arithmetic.

(a) un = Sn - Sn-1
Sn-1 = 2(n-1)2 + 5(n-1) = 2(n2-2n+1) + 5n-5 = 2n2-4n+2+5n-5 = 2n2+n-3
un = (2n2+5n) - (2n2+n-3) = 4n+3
(b) un+1 - un = 4(n+1)+3 - (4n+3) = 4, constant ⇒ arithmetic
M1 for un=Sn-Sn-1, A1 for 4n+3, M1 for difference, A1 for constant 4

Question 2.7c Advanced7 marks

A geometric series has first term a = 24 and sum to infinity of 36.
(a) Find the common ratio r.
(b) Find the smallest value of n for which Sn exceeds 35.5.

(a) S∞ = a/(1-r) = 24/(1-r) = 36
24 = 36(1-r) ⇒ 24 = 36 - 36r ⇒ 36r = 12 ⇒ r = 1/3
(b) Sn = a(1-rn)/(1-r) = 24(1-(1/3)n)/(2/3) = 36(1-(1/3)n)
36(1-(1/3)n) > 35.5 ⇒ 1-(1/3)n > 35.5/36 = 71/72
(1/3)n < 1/72 ⇒ n ln(3) > ln(72) ⇒ n > ln72/ln3 = 3.89
Smallest n = 4
M1 for S∞ formula, A1 for r=1/3, M1 for Sn formula, M1 for inequality, A1 for n=4

Question 2.7d Advanced6 marks

A sequence is defined by un+1 = 3un - 2 with u1 = 4.

(c) Prove your formula by induction.

Examiner Tips — By Topic

These tips are distilled from analysing WJEC mark schemes (2018-2025). They highlight exactly what examiners look for and common mistakes to avoid.

Algebraic Fractions & Polynomial Division
• Always factorise fully before cancelling - examiners check for highest common factors, not just obvious ones
• When dividing polynomials, use the factor theorem first to check if the divisor is a factor. If f(a)=0, (x-a) is a factor
• In algebraic long division, write 0 coefficients for missing powers (e.g., x3+0x2+...)
• Quotient and remainder: degree of remainder < degree of divisor
• Common error: Cancelling terms instead of factors (e.g., cancelling x from numerator and denominator of (x2+3)/x → x+3 is WRONG)
Partial Fractions
• For linear denominators: A/(x-a)+B/(x-b). Cover-up method works for linear factors only
• For repeated linear: A/(x-a)+B/(x-a)2
• For quadratic denominator: (Bx+C)/(ax2+bx+c)
• Always check the degree of the numerator - if degree ≥ degree of denominator, polynomial division first
• Common error: Forgetting the C term in (Bx+C)/(quadratic). B alone is not enough
Modulus Function
• |f(x)|=g(x): solve f(x)=g(x) and -f(x)=g(x). Check solutions satisfy original equation
• |f(x)|<g(x): solve -g(x)<f(x)<g(x)
• |f(x)|>g(x): solve f(x)<-g(x) OR f(x)>g(x)
• Squaring both sides is efficient for |f(x)|=|g(x)| or |f(x)|<|g(x)|
• Common error: Forgetting to check solutions. Squaring can introduce extraneous solutions
Functions (Domain, Range, Inverse, Composite)
• Domain: avoid division by zero, square roots of negatives, logs of non-positive numbers
• Range: find by considering the behaviour of the function across its domain (including endpoints and turning points)
• For inverse: swap x and y, rearrange, check domain of inverse = range of original
• Composite fg(x): apply g first, then f. Domain: x must be in domain of g AND g(x) must be in domain of f
• Common error: Domain of f-1 = range of f, NOT the domain of f. Students regularly get this wrong
Transformations
• Order matters: y=af(x+b)+c: horizontal shift, then stretch, then vertical shift
• y=f(ax): horizontal stretch by 1/a (this is counterintuitive - many students get it backwards)
• y=-f(x): reflection in x-axis. y=f(-x): reflection in y-axis
• Common error: y=f(x+2) moves left, not right. "Inside the brackets, opposite direction"
Proof by Contradiction
• Structure: Assume the opposite → derive a contradiction → conclude original statement is true
• For irrational proofs: assume rational p/q in lowest terms, show p and q share a factor (contradiction)
• For "greatest" proofs: construct a larger element of the same type
• Common error: Not explicitly stating the contradiction or the concluding statement. The final "Therefore..." line earns a mark
Series
• Arithmetic: a+(n-1)d; Sn=n/2(2a+(n-1)d)=n/2(a+l)
• Geometric: arn-1; Sn=a(1-rn)/(1-r); S∞=a/(1-r) for |r|<1
• For recurrence: un+1=f(un), find pattern then prove by induction
• Sn-Sn-1=un - useful for finding nth term from sum formula
• Common error: Using S∞ formula when |r|≥1 (diverges). Always check |r|<1 first
Binomial Series (Fractional/Negative Index)
• Always rewrite as (something)(1+kx)n before expanding
• Formula: (1+x)n=1+nx+n(n-1)x2/2!+n(n-1)(n-2)x3/3!+...
• Validity: |kx|<1 → |x|<1/|k|
• When combined with partial fractions, add the expansions term by term
• Common error: Forgetting the range of validity. This is almost always worth a mark. State it explicitly
Radian Measure
• Arc length s=rθ. Area of sector A=½r2θ
• Area of triangle in sector = ½r2 sin θ
• Area of segment = sector area - triangle area
• Always check your calculator is in RADIAN mode for all trig working
• Common error: Forgetting to convert degrees to radians before using arc length/sector area formulae
Small Angle Approximations
• For small θ (radians): sin θ ≈ θ, cos θ ≈ 1-θ2/2, tan θ ≈ θ
• Expand brackets fully and simplify - cancellations often occur
• Be careful with multiples: sin kθ ≈ kθ, cos kθ ≈ 1-(kθ)2/2
• Common error: Using sin θ ≈ θ for large θ - approximation only valid for θ near 0
Reciprocal Trig Functions
• sec x=1/cos x, cosec x=1/sin x, cot x=1/tan x=cos x/sin x
• Graphs: sec and cosec have vertical asymptotes where cos/sin = 0
• Derivatives: d(sec x)/dx=sec x tan x, d(cosec x)/dx=-cosec x cot x, d(cot x)/dx=-cosec2x
• Integrals: ∫sec2x dx=tan x+C, ∫cosec2x dx=-cot x+C, ∫sec x tan x dx=sec x+C
• Common error: Confusing the domains: sec x=1/cos x → undefined when cos x=0
Trig Equations
• Get one trig function alone first, then find all solutions in the given range
• Use the quadrant diagram (CAST) to find all solutions systematically
• For quadratic forms (e.g., sin2x+sin x-2=0): factorise and solve each factor separately
• For tan x=k: principal value in (-π/2, π/2), then add π to find others
• Common error: Missing solutions. For sin x=k in 0-360°, there are usually 2 solutions. For tan x=k, there are usually 2 solutions in 0-360°
Compound & Double Angles
• sin(A±B)=sinAcosB±cosAsinB, cos(A±B)=cosAcosB−sinAsinB, tan(A±B)=(tanA±tanB)/(1−tanAtanB)
• Double angles: sin2A=2sinAcosA, cos2A=cos2A-sin2A=2cos2A-1=1-2sin2A
• cos2A=2cos2A-1 gives cos2A=(1+cos2A)/2 (useful for integration)
• cos2A=1-2sin2A gives sin2A=(1-cos2A)/2 (useful for integration)
• Common error: Sign errors in cos(A-B)=cosAcosB+sinAsinB (the sign is OPPOSITE to what many expect)
Harmonic Form (R cos / R sin)
• a cos x + b sin x = R cos(x-α) where R=√(a2+b2), cos α=a/R, sin α=b/R
• a sin x + b cos x = R sin(x+α) where R=√(a2+b2), sin α=b/R, cos α=a/R
• Max value=R, min value=-R
• When solving R cos(x-α)=k, general solution: x-α=±cos-1(k/R)+2nπ
• Common error: Forgetting to adjust ALL solutions by adding/subtracting α after finding them
Differentiation Rules
• Chain: dy/dx=dy/du×du/dx. Useful for functions of functions
• Product: d(uv)/dx=u dv/dx+v du/dx
• Quotient: d(u/v)/dx=(v du/dx-u dv/dx)/v2
• Write "u=..., v=..." explicitly - examiners award method marks even if final answer is wrong
• Common error: In quotient rule, getting the minus sign wrong. "vdu-udv over v-squared, in that order"
Implicit Differentiation
• Differentiate x terms normally, y terms with dy/dx attached
• For xy: use product rule: y+x dy/dx
• For y2: 2y dy/dx (chain rule)
• Collect all dy/dx terms on one side, factorise
• Common error: Forgetting the dy/dx when differentiating y terms (e.g., differentiating y2 to 2y instead of 2y dy/dx)
Parametric Differentiation
• dy/dx = (dy/dt) ÷ (dx/dt)
• d2y/dx2 = d/dx(dy/dx) = d/dt(dy/dx) ÷ (dx/dt)
• For tangents: find dy/dx at the point, use y-y0=m(x-x0)
• For normals: gradient = -1/(dy/dx) at the point
• Common error: Writing dy/dx=dx/dt÷dy/dt (inverted). Always: parametric derivative=(dy/dt)/(dx/dt)
Points of Inflection
• Condition: d2y/dx2=0 AND d2y/dx2 changes sign (or d3y/dx3≠0)
• Check sign change either side - necessary for full marks
• A point of inflection is where the curve changes from concave to convex (or vice versa)
• Common error: Just solving d2y/dx2=0 without checking sign change. A stationary point with d2y/dx2=0 is not guaranteed to be an inflection!
Integration
• Standard integrals: know sin→-cos, cos→sin, ekx→(1/k)ekx, 1/x→ln|x|
• Substitution: change limits when doing definite integrals (don't back-substitute)
• Parts: ∫u dv/dx dx = uv - ∫v du/dx dx. Choose u wisely (LATE rule: Logs, Algebra, Trig, Exponentials)
• For ∫xnexdx: multiple rounds of parts needed
• Common error: Forgetting the constant of integration +C for indefinite integrals. This costs a mark every time
Area Between Curves
• Area = ∫(upper-lower) dx between intersection points
• Find intersection points first (set equations equal)
• For parametric curves: Area = ∫y dx = ∫y(dx/dt) dt
• Always draw a quick sketch to determine which curve is above
• Common error: Integrating the wrong way round (e.g., lower-upper gives negative area). Take absolute value or sketch first
Differential Equations
• Separation of variables: get all y terms with dy, all x terms with dx
• Integrate both sides, add constant to one side only
• Use initial condition to find the constant
• Express y explicitly as a function of x where possible
• Common error: Forgetting the constant of integration, or not using the initial condition to find its value
Numerical Methods
• Sign change: show f(a)×f(b)<0 and state continuity
• Newton-Raphson: xn+1=xn-f(xn)/f'(xn). Iterate until convergence to required dp
• Iteration: rearrange equation to x=g(x) form, use xn+1=g(xn)
• Trapezium Rule: h/2[(y0+yn)+2(y1+...+yn-1)], with h=(b-a)/n
• Common error: In the trapezium rule, forgetting to multiply the middle terms by 2, or using the wrong h
General Exam Technique
• Show ALL working - WJEC awards method marks even for incorrect final answers
• For "show that" questions, reproduce every step clearly. Don't skip algebraic simplifications
• Check degrees/radians mode! This is the #1 avoidable error
• 2hr30min for 120 marks → about 1.25 minutes per mark. Use time proportionally
• Answer every question - even partial working earns marks
• Read the question twice before starting. Note which parts are linked (e.g., "(a) hence" means use (a) in (b))
• For exact answers: leave in surd/fraction form unless told otherwise
• For 3 sf answers: only round the final answer, not intermediate steps
• Last tip: The most common mark lost in WJEC Unit 3 is the final A1 for not stating units or not rounding to the specified degree of accuracy
